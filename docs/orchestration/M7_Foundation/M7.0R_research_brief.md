## 1. RL under Partial Observability

### Source

Kaelbling, L.P., Littman, M.L., Cassandra, A.R. (1998).
"Planning and Acting in Partially Observable Stochastic Domains."
*Artificial Intelligence* 101(1–2):99–134.

### Grounded claims

The Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) is the correct formal framework
when an agent cannot observe the complete system state. In a POMDP, observations are a
stochastic function of the underlying state — noisy, delayed, or restricted in scope. Policy
performance is bounded by the information content of the observation relative to the
state variables that drive reward.

A policy trained on full state information will not transfer to partial-observation deployment
without modification, because the policy may exploit state variables that are unavailable at
deployment.

### Relevance to obs14_v1

obs14_v1 contains two classes of inputs:
- Indices 0–9: truth-derived (full-state, simulation-only)
- Indices 10–13: sensor-derived (noisy partial observations of the physical state)

The obs14_v1 policy is trained in a mixed regime: part full-state, part partial observation.
This is not a POMDP in the strict deployment sense — the policy exploits truth channels (0–9)
that will not be available at hardware deployment. The training regime is formally an MDP
(the policy's full input at each step is the training observation vector), but the
deployment scenario is a POMDP.

**Architecture decision (internal):** Retaining truth channels during M7 baseline training
is an internal HydrOS decision. It is the simplest approach for a first baseline and
defers deployment-mode obs design to a future phase. This is not directly mandated by
POMDP theory.

## 2. Sensor Noise and Delayed Observations in RL

### Source

Dulac-Arnold, G., Levine, N., Mankowitz, D.J., et al. (2021).
"Challenges of real-world reinforcement learning: definitions, benchmarks and analysis."
*Machine Learning* 110(9):2419–2468. DOI: 10.1007/s10994-021-05961-4.
(Preprint: arXiv:1904.12901, 2019.)

### Grounded claims

Dulac-Arnold et al. (2021) identify delayed observations (Challenge 7) and partial
observability (Challenge 9) as independent real-world RL deployment challenges. Both violate
assumptions built into standard MDP algorithms (PPO, SAC, etc.).

On observation delay: "The naïve approach is to treat the problem as an MDP and hope that
the policy learns to cope with the delay implicitly." The authors note this approach works
acceptably for short delays (approximately 1 step) but degrades as delay increases. Explicit
delay handling (via state augmentation with history, or recurrent architectures) improves
performance under longer delays.

On sensor noise: if the noise model used during training is miscalibrated relative to the
real system, the deployed policy will face an observation distribution shift. The magnitude
of this shift determines how much the trained policy degrades at deployment.

### Relevance to obs14_v1

- `dp_sensor_kPa` (index 13) has a verified 1-step latency (M6.1, `test_dp_latency_one_step`).
  This is a 1-step observation delay on one of the two new sensor channels.
- `flow_sensor_lmin` (index 12) has per-episode calibration bias (std = 0.2 L/min) and AWGN
  (1% reading). No delay, but episode-to-episode noise envelope shifts.
- Sensor noise parameters (`dp_drift_rate_kPa_per_step = 0.0005`, `dp_fouling_gain = 0.5`)
  are calibration-pending placeholders. They are not calibrated to physical measurements.

**Architecture decision (internal):** For the M7 baseline, a MLP policy is trained under the
standard MDP assumption, treating the 1-step dp_sensor delay implicitly. This is supported
by Dulac-Arnold et al.'s observation that 1-step delays are manageable without explicit
handling. A recurrent architecture (LSTM/GRU) would be more principled but adds training
complexity; it is deferred to post-baseline.

**Known baseline limitation (calibration-pending):** The noise model used in M7 training
reflects placeholder parameter values. Future calibration updates will shift the sensor
observation distribution. The M7 baseline policy will need to be retrained or re-evaluated
after calibration. This is expected and not a blocking concern.

## 3. Sim-to-Real Transfer

### Sources

**S1:** Tobin, J., Fong, R., Ray, A., Schneider, J., Zaremba, W., Abbeel, P. (2017).
"Domain Randomization for Transferring Deep Neural Networks from Simulation to the
Real World." *Proc. IEEE/RSJ IROS 2017*. arXiv:1703.06907.

**S2:** Peng, X.B., Andrychowicz, M., Zaremba, W., Abbeel, P. (2018).
"Sim-to-Real Transfer of Robotic Control with Dynamics Randomization."
*Proc. IEEE ICRA 2018*. arXiv:1710.06537.

### Grounded claims

Tobin et al. (2017) demonstrate that randomizing simulator parameters (appearance,
lighting, object properties) during training — domain randomization — can cause the real
world to appear as just another variation of the simulator. With sufficient variability,
policies trained entirely in simulation transfer zero-shot to real systems.

Peng et al. (2018) extend this principle to physics dynamics: randomizing mass, friction,
and actuator response across training episodes produces policies that adapt to the real
system's dynamics without real-world training. Critically, the real-world parameter values
must fall within the randomization range used during training. If the real system lies
outside the trained distribution, transfer fails.

Both papers establish the principle: **calibration informs the useful range of
randomization.** Without calibrated parameter bounds, domain randomization either
under-covers (too narrow → fails on real system) or over-covers (too wide → policy
degrades due to irrelevant dynamics).

### Relevance to obs14_v1

HydrionEnv sensor noise parameters are calibration-pending. The current placeholder values:
- Are fixed (not randomized) during training
- Are not validated against physical measurements
- May not bracket the actual device operating range

Training under fixed placeholder noise = training on a single noise-model estimate.
This is weaker than proper domain randomization per Tobin et al. and Peng et al., and
carries a known distribution-shift risk at deployment.

**Architecture decision (internal):** Calibration-aware domain randomization of sensor
parameters is deferred until physical calibration provides credible bounds. The M7 baseline
trains under fixed placeholder noise. This is a documented limitation, not a failure of
process — the baseline's purpose is to establish a training pipeline and performance reference
point, not to demonstrate deployment robustness.

## 4. Privileged Information Training

### Sources

**S1:** Loquercio, A., Kaufmann, E., Ranftl, R., Müller, M., Koltun, V., Scaramuzza, D.
(2021). "Learning high-speed flight in the wild." *Science Robotics* 6(59), eabg5810.
DOI: 10.1126/scirobotics.abg5810. arXiv:2110.05113.

**S2:** Kumar, A., et al. (2021). "RMA: Rapid Motor Adaptation for Legged Robots."
arXiv:2107.04034.

### Grounded claims

Loquercio et al. (2021) train a sensorimotor policy via privileged learning: a teacher
with access to full depth information distills into a student that operates on RGB camera
input only. The teacher-student distillation step is what enables deployment with partial
observations. Zero-shot sim-to-real transfer is achieved by simulating realistic sensor
noise during student training.

Kumar et al. (2021) train a base policy with access to privileged environment parameters
(terrain type, friction, mass), then train a separate adaptation module to estimate those
parameters from recent state-action history. At deployment, the adaptation module replaces
the privileged input. The base policy + adaptation module is the deployed system.

In both cases, there is an explicit mechanism for handling the gap between the privileged
training environment and the partial-observation deployment environment: either distillation
(Loquercio) or an adaptation module (Kumar).

### Relevance to obs14_v1

obs14_v1 retains truth-derived indices 0–9 alongside sensor-derived indices 10–13 in the
same policy input vector. This is a form of privileged information training: the policy
has access to perfect state information that will not be present at deployment.

However, obs14_v1 does **not** implement a teacher-student or adaptation-module architecture.
Truth channels are simply included in the training input vector. There is no distillation
step and no mechanism to handle their absence at deployment. The M7 baseline policy will
be trained with and will depend on indices 0–9. Deploying it without those channels requires
either sensor estimation of the truth quantities or retraining without them.

**Architecture decision (internal):** The M7 baseline does not implement teacher-student
distillation or an adaptation module. The dual-channel obs14_v1 design is consistent with
the general motivation behind privileged information training (train with more information
than is available at deployment) but does not implement the specific mechanisms those papers
describe. This must be stated explicitly in the execution document: truth indices 0–9 are
privileged training signals. Deployment-mode obs design is a future phase.

## 5. Artifact Comparability When Observation Schema Changes

### Literature coverage

There is **no peer-reviewed standard** for RL observation schema versioning or artifact
comparability across schema changes. This is a recognized gap in the RL systems literature.

**Relevant analogy:** Semantic versioning (semver.org) governs software interface
compatibility. The principle that an incompatible interface change requires a major version
bump maps to RL obs schemas: a change in observation dimensionality creates an architectural
incompatibility at the policy input layer.

**Documented practice in the codebase:** The change-from-obs12_v2 note in
`sensor_fusion.py`, the schema version label in metadata JSON, and the CCE guard test
follow this principle and are analogous to major-version discipline for software interfaces.

### What can be stated from RL systems practice

A policy trained on a k-dimensional observation cannot be evaluated on a j-dimensional
(j ≠ k) observation without modification. Any padding or truncation produces an
out-of-distribution input; any performance metric recorded under those conditions is not
a valid benchmark comparison.

VecNormalize statistics (running mean and variance of observations) are schema-specific.
Statistics computed over obs12_v2 (12D) cannot be applied to obs14_v1 (14D) observations:
different dimensionality, different distribution. New statistics must be computed from
scratch for every schema version.

Cross-environment comparisons (CCE vs HydrionEnv) require independent justification beyond
schema versioning. CCE and HydrionEnv have different physics pipelines, different reward
functions, and different operating envelopes, in addition to different obs schemas.

**Architecture decision (internal):** The obs schema versioning discipline (obs14_v1 label,
metadata JSON schema field, artifact naming with schema suffix) is an internal HydrOS
architecture decision. It is not sourced from a published standard.
