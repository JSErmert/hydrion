## 2. Sensor Type Definitions

### 2.1 Differential Pressure Sensor

**Likely real-device class:** Piezoresistive silicon differential pressure transducer (lab-scale) or capacitive/piezoresistive industrial dp transmitter (pilot-scale). Used to measure the pressure drop across the filter element.

**Operating principle:** A sensing diaphragm deforms under applied differential pressure; the deformation is read out via piezoresistive strain gauges (voltage proportional to deformation) or as a capacitance change. The readout is temperature-compensated electronically and typically produces a 4–20 mA, 0–5V, or digital (I²C/SPI) output.

**Expected use in HydrOS microfluidic / filtration system:**
- Measures `dp_total_pa` across the filter element — the difference between upstream and downstream pressure
- Primary input to inferring filter fouling status (as cake builds, dp rises)
- Often the most-important single sensor in filtration control because the dp rise rate is the primary fouling indicator

**Known noise sources:**
1. **Zero drift** — a slow shift in the zero-pressure reading due to thermal effects, electronic drift, or mechanical stress on the diaphragm
2. **Span drift** — a slow change in the gain (less common than zero drift in modern compensated transducers)
3. **White noise / random uncertainty** — per-reading measurement noise, typically Gaussian, at the 0.01–0.5% of span level depending on device class
4. **Line clogging / impulse-line fouling** — in installations where the pressure is transmitted to the sensor through impulse lines, debris in the lines can cause slow offsets and damped response
5. **Element fouling (not a sensor fault)** — the dp reading rises over time as the filter element fouls; this is the signal, not noise, but is often confused with sensor drift

### 2.2 Flow Sensor / Flow Meter

**Likely real-device class:** Several candidates depending on installation:
- **Ultrasonic flow meter** (transit-time or Doppler) — non-invasive, high accuracy, expensive; no moving parts
- **Coriolis mass flow meter** — highest accuracy, directly measures mass flow; expensive
- **Electromagnetic flow meter** — good accuracy, requires conductive fluid, non-invasive to flow path
- **Turbine flow meter** — mechanical, lower cost, moderate accuracy, wear-prone
- **Paddle / rotameter** — lowest cost, lowest accuracy
- **Thermal mass flow meter** — common in low-flow lab applications

For a lab- or pilot-scale microplastic extraction device, ultrasonic and coriolis are the most likely research-grade choices. Turbine and thermal mass flow are the most likely lower-budget choices.

**Operating principle (varies by type):** Each type has its own physics. They share a common characterization: they produce a flow reading with a characteristic accuracy ± full-scale spec, a calibration curve fit to a reference (water, at a specified temperature), and drift characteristics that depend on mechanical wear (turbine), electronics drift (ultrasonic), or none (coriolis).

**Expected use in HydrOS:**
- Measures volumetric flow rate of the processed water through the filter
- Input to inferring `q_processed` and control of pump/valve actuation
- Secondary role: cross-check against pressure-derived flow inferences

**Known noise sources:**
1. **Calibration bias** — offset between the factory calibration curve and the actual operating point, especially if the operating fluid differs from the calibration fluid (viscosity, temperature, particle content)
2. **Zero offset** — non-zero reading at zero flow, especially for mechanical meters
3. **Measurement noise** — per-reading noise, distribution and magnitude depends on technology
4. **Clogging / particle interference** — for mechanical meters (turbine, paddle), particulates can jam or bias the reading
5. **Drift** — calibration changes over time; low for electronic meters, higher for mechanical
6. **Temperature sensitivity** — most flow meters need temperature compensation

### 2.3 Turbidity / Optical Sensor

**Likely real-device class:** Nephelometric turbidity sensor (side-scatter at 90°) or attenuated-beam transmittance sensor. Lab instruments typically follow ISO 7027 (90° scatter at 860 nm).

**Operating principle:** A light source (LED, typically 860 nm near-infrared) illuminates the sample; a detector measures either the 90° scattered intensity (nephelometric, NTU units) or the transmitted intensity (attenuation, FAU/FNU/FTU units). The reading depends on the concentration of scattering particles, but ALSO on their size distribution, shape, and refractive index.

**Expected use in HydrOS:**
- `sensor_turbidity` — primary nephelometric reading
- `sensor_scatter` — secondary scatter channel (possibly at a different angle or wavelength; exact HydrOS semantics to be confirmed from `sensor_fusion.py`)
- Intended as a proxy for microplastic concentration / particle loading in the output stream

**The fundamental interpretive limit:**

> **Turbidity is NOT a direct measurement of mass concentration.**

Turbidity is a function of `∑(particle_count × scattering_cross_section(size, shape, refractive_index))`. Two suspensions can have the same turbidity while having very different mass concentrations if their particle size distributions differ. For microplastic applications specifically:

- Microplastics span ~1 µm to millimeters in size — scattering behavior varies by orders of magnitude across this range
- Refractive index varies by polymer type (PP, PE, PET all have different RI — M5 material properties confirm this)
- Shape varies wildly (fibers, fragments, beads, films) — non-spherical particles have different scattering signatures
- Formazin-calibrated NTU readings are valid for formazin standard, but converting NTU to mg/L microplastic requires a system-specific empirical calibration

**Implication for M9:** Calibration can ground the turbidity sensor's **noise characteristics** (how noisy is the NTU reading for a given true NTU?) but **cannot ground the NTU-to-microplastic-concentration mapping** without direct experimental correlation against the actual microplastic population in the HydrOS target application. This is an experimental data requirement, not a literature-review question.

This is a pre-existing architectural decision in HydrOS (turbidity used as a proxy) that should be flagged as calibration-pending-by-experiment, not calibration-pending-by-literature.

## 3. Parameter Grounding

**All values below are PRELIMINARY engineering-typical ranges at LOW confidence. None are verified against specific peer-reviewed sources or device datasheets. M9.3 must not use these values as final until a verified-source pass (M9.1R.3) completes.**

### 3.1 `dp_drift_rate`

**Physical meaning:** Rate of change of the zero-pressure reading over time due to thermal/electronic/mechanical drift of the transducer (NOT related to filter fouling — that is the `dp_fouling_gain` parameter).

**Units:** kPa per unit time (per second, per hour, per day — HydrOS-specific unit must be confirmed from `init_sensor_state` / simulation time-scale). Care required: long-term drift specs are usually given per year, but the HydrOS simulation horizon is typically seconds to minutes, so the per-step contribution is small.

**Preliminary engineering-typical ranges (LOW confidence):**

| Device class | Typical long-term zero drift | Per-hour equivalent (rough) |
|-------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Laboratory-grade piezoresistive dp transducer (temperature-compensated) | ~0.05 – 0.2 % of span per year | ~ 6e-6 to 2e-5 % of span per hour |
| Industrial dp transmitter (4–20 mA, high-quality) | ~0.1 – 0.5 % of span per year | ~ 1e-5 to 6e-5 % of span per hour |
| Low-cost resistive dp sensor | ~0.5 – 2 % of span per year or worse | ~ 6e-5 to 2e-4 % of span per hour |

**To translate to HydrOS units:** Multiply by the full scale (`P_max_Pa` = 80,000 Pa = 80 kPa) and by the relevant per-step time increment. Example calculation: a 0.1% span/year drift on an 80 kPa span yields ~0.08 kPa/year ≈ 9e-6 kPa/hr ≈ 2.5e-9 kPa/sec. Over a 400-step episode at 1 Hz, this is ~1e-6 kPa — essentially negligible compared to any meaningful reading. Drift is therefore a long-run calibration concern, not an intra-episode noise source.

**Implication for the M8 result:** If placeholder `dp_drift_rate` in HydrOS was set to a value much larger than the calibrated range, it may have been introducing drift on episode timescales that real sensors do not exhibit. If placeholder was set smaller (or zero), calibration may actually add a small amount of drift the policy now sees. The direction of change on the M8 +21.2% gap depends on the current placeholder value — this needs to be audited in M9.1B (if a parallel audit exists) or in the M9.3 execution document.

**Confidence: LOW. Requires peer-reviewed or datasheet verification.**
**Source category:** General piezoresistive transducer engineering knowledge.

### 3.2 `dp_fouling_gain`

**Physical meaning:** This is where the current HydrOS implementation may need clarification. Two distinct physical processes could be meant:

**(A) Filter element fouling → dp reading rise:** The filter cake builds up, actual differential pressure across the filter rises; the sensor correctly reports this. This is signal, not sensor behavior.

**(B) Impulse-line fouling or sensor-diaphragm fouling → spurious dp offset:** Debris in the line to the sensor or on the diaphragm itself causes the reading to differ from the true pressure. This is sensor error, not signal.

**Current HydrOS interpretation (from memory / inspection of `sensor_fusion.py` semantics):** The parameter is named `dp_fouling_gain` and appears to model how the sensor reading diverges from truth as fouling progresses. This sounds like process (B), but process (A) is the physically dominant effect in filtration.

**Recommendation:** M9.1B (parallel audit) or M9.3 (execution document) must confirm which process `dp_fouling_gain` represents in the current code. The calibration target changes depending on the answer:

- If process (A): `dp_fouling_gain` should probably be **removed or renamed** — the underlying physics model (truth_state) already computes `dp_total_pa` as a function of fouling state. Adding a `dp_fouling_gain` sensor parameter would double-count.
- If process (B): `dp_fouling_gain` is a legitimate sensor-error parameter. Typical real-world magnitude is hard to pin down — impulse-line fouling is installation-specific. Typical ranges are on the order of 1–10% of reading accumulated over months of operation in fouling-prone installations.

**Preliminary engineering-typical ranges (LOW confidence, assuming process B):**

| Installation type | Fouling-induced dp reading error after extended operation |
|-------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| Clean / filtered feed with modern direct-mount transducer | <0.5% of reading per month |
| Standard filtration with impulse lines | 1–5% of reading per month |
| High-fouling / particulate-heavy feed | 5–20% of reading per month or worse |

**On episode timescales (seconds to minutes), sensor-line fouling accumulates negligibly.** The calibrated value should be near-zero for any realistic episode simulation unless HydrOS is explicitly modeling months-long device operation.

**Confidence: LOW. Requires both clarification of HydrOS semantics AND verified source on whichever interpretation holds.**
**Source category:** Sensor installation engineering knowledge; requires confirmation.

### 3.3 `flow_calibration_bias_std`

**Physical meaning:** Standard deviation of the calibration-bias distribution — i.e., the uncertainty in the flow meter's reading after factory calibration, accounting for drift and operating-point mismatch.

**Units:** Presumably normalized or in L/min (HydrOS-specific — must be confirmed).

**Preliminary engineering-typical accuracy by meter type (LOW confidence):**

| Meter type | Typical accuracy (calibrated) | Notes |
|------------|------------------------------|-------|
| Coriolis mass flow | ±0.1 – 0.5% of reading | Gold standard; high cost |
| Ultrasonic (transit-time) | ±0.5 – 2% of reading | Non-invasive; temperature-sensitive |
| Electromagnetic | ±0.5 – 1% of reading | Requires conductive fluid |
| Thermal mass (low-flow lab) | ±1 – 3% of full scale | Common in lab setups |
| Turbine | ±1 – 2% of reading | Wear-prone over time |
| Paddle / rotameter | ±2 – 5% of full scale | Low cost, low accuracy |

**Interpretation as bias standard deviation:** For a meter specified at ±X% of reading, `flow_calibration_bias_std` should approximately be X% of the nominal operating flow. This is a one-sigma estimate; the spec X% may be a 2-sigma or 3-sigma limit depending on vendor convention, which introduces additional uncertainty (factor of 2–3) in the calibration.

**For HydrOS (assuming a research-grade lab setup):** A reasonable preliminary range is `flow_calibration_bias_std ≈ 0.005 – 0.02 × nominal_flow_L_per_min` (i.e., 0.5–2% of reading, 1-sigma). For a nominal ~10 L/min operating point, this is ~0.05–0.2 L/min.

**Confidence: LOW. Requires either peer-reviewed metrology literature OR a specific meter class nomination + datasheet.**
**Source category:** Industrial flow metrology common knowledge; ISO 5167 (orifice plate reference, widely known category) referenced as a standards anchor only.

### 3.4 Turbidity / Scatter Noise Parameters

**Note:** These were not named in the M9.1R.1 prompt as primary calibration targets, but sensor_turbidity and sensor_scatter are in obs8_deployment_v1 and should be mentioned for completeness.

**Physical meaning:** Random and systematic error in the turbidity and scatter readings.

**Preliminary engineering-typical ranges (LOW confidence):**

| Parameter | Typical laboratory instrument | Typical field instrument |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| NTU measurement noise (1-sigma) | ±2–5% of reading | ±5–10% of reading |
| NTU calibration drift | ~1% per month | ~5–10% per month |
| NTU-to-mass calibration | Instrument-dependent; requires experimental correlation | Same |

**The dominant calibration gap is the NTU-to-microplastic-mass mapping, not the NTU noise itself.** This is a fundamental limit, not a research gap — see Section 5.

**Confidence: LOW for noise characteristics; FUNDAMENTAL for NTU-to-mass mapping.**
**Source category:** ISO 7027 (turbidity measurement standard, broadly known); general nephelometric instrumentation knowledge.

## 4. Noise Model Interpretation

### 4.1 What the placeholder noise physically represents (if anything)

The current HydrOS implementation (per inspection of `sensor_fusion.py` from memory) applies `dp_drift_rate`, `dp_fouling_gain`, and `flow_calibration_bias_std` as modifiers on the truth-state readings to produce sensor readings. The placeholder values are not specifically anchored to any sensor class.

**This means:** the current sensor noise model is **shape-correct** (drift + fouling-offset + calibration-bias is a reasonable decomposition) but **magnitude-arbitrary**. The noise does not reflect how any specific real sensor would behave. It reflects a plausible-looking but uncalibrated error model.

### 4.2 Whether current HydrOS interpretation is correct

**Structurally (shape of the noise model):** Mostly correct. The three-parameter decomposition (drift + fouling + bias) is a reasonable approximation of sensor error for short-duration simulation.

**Semantically (what each parameter represents):** Partially uncertain. Specifically:

1. **`dp_drift_rate`** — shape-correct (linear or exponential drift over time). Magnitude likely wrong but direction-correct.
2. **`dp_fouling_gain`** — **potentially semantically confused.** If HydrOS applies this as a sensor error term, but the actual dp rise due to filter cake fouling is already computed in the physics truth state, then this parameter double-counts. M9.3 must resolve this by inspecting the code path. If the parameter is process (A) above, it should be **removed** and the calibrated value should be zero. If it is process (B), a small nonzero value from calibration is appropriate.
3. **`flow_calibration_bias_std`** — shape-correct (Gaussian bias on flow reading). Magnitude likely wrong.

### 4.3 What must change

**Minimum changes for M9 sensor realism:**

1. **Verify `dp_fouling_gain` semantics in code** (M9.3 prerequisite — inspect `sensor_fusion.py` and `init_sensor_state` to confirm whether process A or B is being modeled). This is a code audit, not a research question.
2. **Nominate a sensor device class** for each of the three sensor types. This is a decision the user makes, informed by the research brief. Without this nomination, calibration cannot produce a verified value.
3. **Replace placeholder values with calibrated values** drawn from either peer-reviewed literature (option a) or from device datasheets for the nominated class (option b). M9.3 execution document specifies the exact replacement.
4. **Record provenance for each calibrated value** in the updated config file and in `ppo_hydrienv_v2_cal_meta.json`. Provenance includes: source reference, confidence level, assumptions, and whether the value is direct or derived.
5. **Preserve the placeholder-noise M8 reference** (`ppo_hydrienv_v2.zip`) as the pre-calibration baseline for comparison.

**Maximum changes (optional for M9, likely deferred to M10):**

1. Extend the noise model to include **temperature compensation error**, **response-time lag**, **quantization noise**, and **sensor cross-talk** — all real sensor effects not currently modeled.
2. Add **sensor saturation / clipping behavior** at the physical measurement limits.
3. Add **episodic failure modes** (intermittent dropouts, spikes) — often important for robust policy training.

These are deferred because they require new model structure, not just parameter values. M9 is parameter-value scope.

## 8. Visual Validity Implication

### 8.1 How calibration improves truth-vs-sensor visualization

**Before calibration:** Rendering a truth-vs-sensor comparison in the HydrOS console shows a "truth" trace (peer-reviewed physics) and a "sensor" trace (placeholder noise) side by side. The visual comparison is misleading because the sensor trace's divergence from truth is governed by arbitrary parameters. A viewer who sees the sensor trace drift above or below truth has no way to judge whether the drift is realistic.

**After calibration:** The sensor trace reflects noise characteristics that match a real device class. The truth-vs-sensor divergence represents what a real deployment would observe. The visual comparison becomes scientifically interpretable — a viewer can judge realism by comparing the displayed behavior to known sensor specs.
