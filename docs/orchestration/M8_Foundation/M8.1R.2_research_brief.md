## 1. Research Framing

The exact M8 question this brief supports:

> **What is the quantitative performance cost of removing the 6 genuinely non-deployment
> physics-truth observation channels (obs14_v1 indices 0–5) while retaining the 8
> deployment-available channels (indices 6–13: actuator feedback + sensor-derived), and
> what can and cannot be concluded from that measurement about HydrionEnv deployment
> viability?**

This is an observation-space ablation problem situated within the broader literature on
partial observability in RL and the sim-to-real deployment gap. The research brief covers
four topics that directly bear on M8's design and interpretation choices:

1. How ablation studies on observation spaces should be designed and interpreted
2. Whether retaining controller-known actuator signals (indices 6–9) in the reduced-
   privilege observation is methodologically justified
3. What methods exist for attributing per-channel contribution to policy performance
4. How to frame and constrain claims about the deployment gap

## 2. Observation-Space Ablation Research

### 2.1 What ablation studies establish and do not establish

Ablation studies in deep RL are a standard empirical methodology for assessing the
contribution of design components to overall policy performance. The practice is widely
used but has received limited formal treatment as a methodology distinct from
hyperparameter search.

Andrychowicz et al. (2021) conducted the most systematic large-scale empirical study of
on-policy RL components, examining how observation normalization, network architecture,
and input representation choices affect PPO policy performance across continuous control
tasks. Their key finding relevant to M8: observation normalization (VecNormalize in SB3
terms) has a large and consistent effect on policy performance — without it, many
otherwise valid configurations fail. This is directly relevant because `ppo_hydrienv_v2`
will require a new VecNormalize fit to `obs8_deployment_v1`. The statistics from
`ppo_hydrienv_v1_vecnorm.pkl` must not be reused.

> Andrychowicz, M., Raichuk, A., Stańczyk, P., et al. (2021). "What Matters In
> On-Policy Reinforcement Learning? A Large-Scale Empirical Study." *International
> Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR 2021)*. arXiv:2006.05990.

Greydanus et al. (2018) developed perturbation-based saliency methods for understanding
which regions of Atari game frames contribute most to agent action selection. While their
domain is image-based RL, the underlying principle — that perturbing or removing input
features reveals their contribution to policy behavior — is the foundation of all
observation-space ablation. Their work shows that contribution is often highly localized
and non-obvious, meaning that prior intuitions about which channels matter may be wrong.

> Greydanus, S., Koul, A., Dodge, J., Fern, A. (2018). "Visualizing and Understanding
> Atari Agents." *Proceedings of the 35th International Conference on Machine Learning
> (ICML 2018)*, pp. 2877–2886. arXiv:1711.00138.

### 2.2 Ablation design requirements for interpretable results

From the literature and standard practice, an observation-space ablation study produces
interpretable results when:

1. **The reduced-observation policy is trained from scratch under the same hyperparameters
   and seed regime as the full-observation policy.** Comparing a freshly trained
   `ppo_hydrienv_v2` against the existing `ppo_hydrienv_v1` is only valid if training
   conditions are otherwise matched (same PPO hyperparameters, same episode length, same
   ShieldedEnv config, same seed). Differences in training conditions confound the
   attribution to observation space.

2. **The evaluation protocol is identical.** Both policies must be evaluated on the same
   environment with the same seeds, episode count, and determinism settings. The M7
   eval script provides a template; M8 must extend it to run both policies in the same
   evaluation pass.

3. **The performance delta is reported with variance, not just means.** A mean return
   comparison between two policies trained under different observation spaces is not
   sufficient — variance across seeds matters for interpreting whether a difference is
   real or within noise.

4. **The confound is named.** For M8 specifically: the sensor noise parameters
   (dp_drift_rate, dp_fouling_gain) remain calibration-pending placeholders in both
   `ppo_hydrienv_v1` and `ppo_hydrienv_v2`. Any performance difference between the two
   policies has two possible sources: (a) information loss from removing channels 0–5,
   and (b) noise-quality effects on the sensor channels. These cannot be separated
   without a separate calibration study. This confound must be stated in M8 evaluation
   output.

### 2.3 What performance loss means — cautions

A performance delta from removing channels is not the same as a measure of "importance."
Channel groups that are individually redundant (because another channel carries
overlapping information) will show low marginal performance loss when removed, even if
they carry useful signal. Channel groups that are critical will show high loss. But the
mapping from performance change to "this channel is important / unimportant" is not
straightforward in nonlinear policies:

- Removing channel A might not affect performance because channel B compensates — but
  removing both A and B simultaneously might cause catastrophic failure.
- A policy trained without channel A from the start may learn a qualitatively different
  strategy that does not need A, producing low performance loss through reorganization
  rather than through A being unimportant.

These cautions mean M8's ablation result should be interpreted as: "what does a policy
trained and evaluated under obs8_deployment_v1 achieve relative to obs14_v1?" — not as
"channels 0–5 contribute X% to policy performance."

**[Internal HydrOS architecture decision]:** The M8 ablation will compare one obs14_v1
policy against one obs8_deployment_v1 policy, both trained from seed=42, same
hyperparameters. This is a practical scope decision, not a methodological standard.
Multi-seed training across both schemas would strengthen the comparison but is out of
M8 scope.

## 3. Action-History / Actuator-Feedback Research

### 3.1 Theoretical justification: controller self-knowledge in POMDPs

In a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), the agent cannot observe the
full underlying state but can observe a stochastic function of it plus — critically — its
own action history. The POMDP information state (or belief state) is defined over the
full history of observations and actions. Actions are unambiguously self-known to the
agent because the agent generates them.

> Kaelbling, L.P., Littman, M.L., Cassandra, A.R. (1998). "Planning and Acting in
> Partially Observable Stochastic Domains." *Artificial Intelligence* 101(1–2):99–134.

This is the foundational theoretical basis for including obs14_v1 indices 6–9 (actuator
commands) in `obs8_deployment_v1`. At each timestep, the controller has issued valve_cmd,
pump_cmd, bf_cmd, and node_voltage_cmd. These are not hidden state — they are
self-generated and exactly known. Including them in the observation is not an
approximation; it is the correct POMDP formulation of a controlled physical system.

### 3.2 Empirical practice: recurrent networks and action history for POMDPs

Hausknecht and Stone (2015) demonstrated that Deep Recurrent Q-Networks (DRQN) with
LSTM layers can handle partial observability in Atari games by learning over observation
histories. A key implication of their work is that history (past observations and actions)
is a sufficient information state for many practical POMDP problems. Their architecture
processes sequences of (observation, action) pairs — the action is always included as
part of the history fed to the recurrent unit.

> Hausknecht, M., Stone, P. (2015). "Deep Recurrent Q-Learning for Partially Observable
> MDPs." *AAAI Fall Symposia 2015*. arXiv:1507.06527.

For a non-recurrent MLP policy (as used in `ppo_hydrienv_v1` and `ppo_hydrienv_v2`),
action history is not explicitly accessible across steps. However, the current-step
actuator command values in obs[6–9] provide the most recent control intent, which is a
weaker but still valid partial substitute for action history — the policy knows what it
commanded last step via the observation of its own output. This is standard practice in
continuous control: including the previous action in the current observation is a common
method for handling one-step information latency without adding recurrent architecture.

### 3.3 Asymmetric actor-critic: formalization of privileged vs deployment-available distinction

Pinto et al. (2018) introduced the asymmetric actor-critic framework: during training,
the critic has access to privileged state information (full state), while the actor uses
only the deployment-available observation. The policy (actor) trained in this asymmetric
setting can be deployed without modification because it was always conditioned on
deployment-available observations.

> Pinto, L., Andrychowicz, M., Welinder, P., Zaremba, W., Abbeel, P. (2018).
> "Asymmetric Actor Critic for Image-Based Robot Learning." *Robotics: Science and
> Systems (RSS 2018)*. arXiv:1710.06542.

This framework formalizes the distinction M8 is making: there is a class of inputs
available only during simulation (physics truth: indices 0–5) and a class available at
deployment (actuator feedback: 6–9, sensor-derived: 10–13). M8's `obs8_deployment_v1`
schema corresponds exactly to the actor's observation space in an asymmetric actor-critic
setting, even though `ppo_hydrienv_v2` will be a standard (non-asymmetric) PPO trained
entirely on the deployment-available observation.

**[Source-derived conclusion]:** Retaining actuator command indices 6–9 in
`obs8_deployment_v1` is not just acceptable — it is the methodologically correct choice.
Removing them would create a more restrictive-than-deployment scenario that conflates the
deployment gap with controller self-knowledge removal.

### 3.4 Action history and adaptation in legged locomotion

Kumar et al. (2021) provides a particularly relevant model for M8. In RMA (Rapid Motor
Adaptation), the base policy is trained with privileged environment factors (terrain,
friction, mass) concatenated to the actor's observation. An adaptation module is then
trained using only the history of proprioceptive observations and actions — identical in
spirit to what obs8_deployment_v1 provides: controller-known actuator history plus
available sensor readings.

> Kumar, A., Fu, Z., Pathak, D., Malik, J. (2021). "RMA: Rapid Motor Adaptation for
> Legged Robots." *Robotics: Science and Systems (RSS 2021)*. arXiv:2107.04034.

The RMA result is relevant as existence proof: an 8D-class deployment-realistic
observation regime can achieve near-privileged performance when the available signals
include controller action history. M8 is not implementing RMA; but the result establishes
that retaining action history (including actuator commands) is methodologically sound
and practically effective.

## 4. Channel-Value / Contribution Research

### 4.1 Perturbation-based attribution

The most accessible method for attributing policy behavior to specific observation channels
is perturbation: zero out, randomize, or hold constant specific channels and observe the
resulting change in policy performance or action distribution. Greydanus et al. (2018)
applied this principle to image-based RL. For vector observations (as in obs14_v1 and
obs8_deployment_v1), the method reduces to: compare policy performance when a channel or
channel group is replaced by its mean value (noise ablation) or zero.

This is methodologically valid for comparative attribution but has the limitation noted in
Section 2.3: it measures marginal contribution, not absolute importance, and is confounded
by channel correlations.

### 4.2 Practical attribution via training ablation

For M8, the most interpretable attribution method is training ablation: train separate
policies with and without specific channel groups and compare learned-policy performance.
This is what M8 does by comparing `ppo_hydrienv_v2` (without channels 0–5) against
`ppo_hydrienv_v1` (with all 14). The result is an end-to-end attribution: the performance
delta measures the total effect of removing channels 0–5 on the best policy the system
can learn, not just the instantaneous effect on a pre-trained policy.

Training ablation is generally considered the strongest form of attribution in RL because
it allows the policy to adapt its strategy to the new information regime rather than
being evaluated with channels artificially zeroed out.

### 4.3 What attribution results do not prove

Channel attribution in RL does not support strong causal claims. A result showing
"ppo_hydrienv_v2 achieves X% of ppo_hydrienv_v1's return" does not prove:
- That channels 0–5 are necessary for good control
- That a sensor-only policy could not learn an equivalent strategy if training were
  extended or hyperparameters were tuned differently
- That the missing channels are responsible for any particular failure mode

**[Internal HydrOS architecture decision]:** M8 will produce one data point (one training
run per schema, same seed) at a fixed hyperparameter configuration. This is sufficient to
establish an M8 baseline but not to draw strong conclusions about the general
deployability of the HydrionEnv control problem.

## 5. Deployment-Gap Framing Research

### 5.1 Teacher-student and privileged information in sim-to-real

Lee et al. (2020) trained a quadrupedal locomotion policy using a teacher policy with
privileged terrain information, then distilled it into a student policy using only
proprioceptive sensors (joint positions, velocities, contact forces — the robot's own
body state). The student policy achieves near-teacher performance on challenging terrain.
The key architectural distinction: the student policy only receives self-available
information, not environment-state information that would require external sensing.

> Lee, J., Hwangbo, J., Wellhausen, L., Koltun, V., Hutter, M. (2020). "Learning
> Quadrupedal Locomotion over Challenging Terrain." *Science Robotics* 5(47),
> eabc5986. DOI: 10.1126/scirobotics.abc5986. arXiv:2010.11251.

This is the most direct precedent for M8. In Lee et al., the teacher-student gap is
analogous to the ppo_hydrienv_v1 vs ppo_hydrienv_v2 gap: one policy uses privileged
simulation state, the other uses only deployment-available observations. The result shows
that deployment-available observations can support near-equivalent control in at least
some physical systems.

**Caution for HydrOS:** Lee et al. use DAgger-based distillation (the student learns to
mimic the teacher). M8 trains `ppo_hydrienv_v2` from scratch on `obs8_deployment_v1`,
not via distillation from `ppo_hydrienv_v1`. This is a simpler approach but may produce
a larger performance gap because the student policy cannot learn from teacher demonstrations.

### 5.2 Massively parallel training with privileged state

Rudin et al. (2022) demonstrated that policies trained with massive parallel simulation
can achieve competitive performance on deployment-available observations when training
compute is increased sufficiently. Their key insight: with enough training steps and
environments, policies can learn to exploit the deployment-available signals without
requiring privileged state, if those signals carry sufficient information.

> Rudin, N., Hoeller, D., Reist, P., Hutter, M. (2022). "Learning to Walk in Minutes
> Using Massively Parallel Deep Reinforcement Learning." *Conference on Robot Learning
> (CoRL 2022)*, Proceedings of Machine Learning Research, vol. 164. arXiv:2109.11978.

For M8, this implies: if `ppo_hydrienv_v2` shows significantly degraded performance
compared to `ppo_hydrienv_v1`, one cause could be insufficient training steps — not
necessarily fundamental information insufficiency. M8's 500k step training budget is
fixed (same as ppo_hydrienv_v1); performance degradation may partly reflect training
efficiency under the reduced observation rather than deployment impossibility.

### 5.3 Challenges of real-world RL and observation regime gaps

Dulac-Arnold et al. (2021) enumerate the challenges of real-world RL, including
observation noise, partial observability, and the mismatch between simulated and real
observation distributions. Their taxonomy distinguishes between:
- Observation noise (sensors are noisy but available)
- Observation limitations (sensors are absent or provide fundamentally different signals)
- Observation lag (sensors are delayed)

obs8_deployment_v1 addresses the "observation limitations" category: channels 0–5 are
not degraded versions of the true physics state — they are categories of information
absent at deployment entirely.

> Dulac-Arnold, G., Levine, N., Mankowitz, D.J., et al. (2021). "Challenges of
> real-world reinforcement learning: definitions, benchmarks and analysis." *Machine
> Learning* 110(9):2419–2468. DOI: 10.1007/s10994-021-05961-4.

### 5.4 Claims to avoid when comparing privileged and reduced-privilege policies

Based on the literature reviewed, the following claims should not be made from M8 results:

1. **"ppo_hydrienv_v2 is deployment-ready."** The sensor noise parameters remain
   calibration-pending placeholders. The obs8_deployment_v1 policy has not been tested
   on real hardware noise profiles.

2. **"Channels 0–5 are unnecessary for good control."** A reduced-privilege policy
   achieving near-equivalent performance does not prove the physics channels are
   unnecessary — it proves they can be substituted by deployment-available signals
   under the training conditions used.

3. **"ppo_hydrienv_v2 proves sensor-only control sufficiency."** obs8_deployment_v1
   includes actuator feedback channels 6–9, which are controller-known signals. This
   is not a sensor-only observation — it is a deployment-realistic observation that
   includes both sensors and actuator history.

4. **"ppo_hydrienv_v2 is better than ppo_hydrienv_v1."** The two policies train on
   different observation spaces. A higher return from v2 would not mean it is
   "better" — it would mean the reduced observation is not limiting at the current
   training scale. The comparison measures a gap, not an improvement.

## 6. HydrOS-Specific Implications

### 6.1 obs8_deployment_v1 design

The decision to define `obs8_deployment_v1` as indices 6–13 (actuator feedback + sensor-
derived) is:

- **[Source-derived]:** Consistent with the POMDP principle (Kaelbling et al. 1998)
  that controller-known actions are part of the information state.
- **[Source-derived]:** Consistent with asymmetric actor-critic practice (Pinto et al.
  2018) that separates deployment-available actor obs from privileged critic obs.
- **[Source-derived]:** Consistent with the teacher-student literature (Lee et al. 2020,
  Kumar et al. 2021) where proprioceptive/self-available signals are the student's input.
- **[Internal HydrOS decision]:** Calling the resulting schema `obs8_deployment_v1` and
  choosing to train a new policy from scratch rather than using distillation.

### 6.2 Decision to retain channels 6–9

Retaining actuator command channels (valve_cmd, pump_cmd, bf_cmd, node_voltage_cmd) in
`obs8_deployment_v1` is:

- **[Source-derived]:** Justified by POMDP theory — the controller always knows its own
  actions.
- **[Source-derived]:** Standard practice — including the previous action as an observation
  is established in continuous control RL (Hausknecht & Stone 2015; Kumar et al. 2021).
- **[Source-derived]:** Necessary for deployment accuracy — a scenario that removes 6–9
  would test a more restrictive setting than any realistic hardware deployment.

There is no literature finding that argues against retaining controller-issued actuator
state in the actor's observation. The question is not whether to retain them but whether
they carry sufficient signal to partially compensate for the removed physics truth channels.
That is an empirical question M8 will answer.

### 6.3 Decision to remove channels 0–5

Removing physics truth channels (flow, pressure, clog, E_field_norm, C_out,
particle_capture_eff) is:

- **[Source-derived]:** Required for deployment realism — these are simulation-computed
  quantities unavailable at hardware deployment, analogous to the terrain information
  removed in Lee et al. 2020 and the environment factors removed in Kumar et al. 2021.
- **[Internal HydrOS decision]:** The specific 6-channel boundary (0–5 are physics truth,
  6–9 are actuator commands) was established in M8.0 refinement by inspecting
  `sensor_fusion.py` and was not present as an explicit classification in M7 metadata.

### 6.4 How M8 comparisons should be interpreted

The `ppo_hydrienv_v2` vs `ppo_hydrienv_v1` comparison should be interpreted as:

> "The performance delta measures the cost of operating the HydrionEnv control task
> with only deployment-available observations, under placeholder sensor noise, at
> 500k training steps, with a fixed PPO hyperparameter configuration."

The following factors bound what the comparison can claim:
- Fixed training steps (500k) — insufficient training may depress v2 performance
- Placeholder sensor noise — v2 uses the same calibration-pending sensor models as v1
- Single seed (seed=42 for training) — no cross-seed variance estimate
- No distillation — v2 trains from scratch, not guided by v1 demonstrations

### 6.5 What M8 should claim

**Claimable after M8:**
- Whether a deployment-realistic policy (`ppo_hydrienv_v2` / `obs8_deployment_v1`) can
  exceed random baseline performance under HydrionEnv
- The magnitude of the performance delta between `obs14_v1` and `obs8_deployment_v1`
  under matched training conditions
- Whether sensor-derived channels remain active and distinct in `ppo_hydrienv_v2` evaluation
- That a deployment-realistic HydrionEnv policy baseline now exists

**Not claimable after M8:**
- Deployment readiness (calibration and hardware testing not done)
- Sensor-only sufficiency (obs8 includes actuator feedback, not just sensors)
- Optimality of obs8_deployment_v1 (other schema designs not tested)
- Generalization across environments, noise regimes, or operating conditions

## 8. Literature-Thin Areas

### 8.1 Observation ablation methodology for continuous flow-control RL

There is no published literature specifically on observation ablation in RL-controlled
membrane filtration or microfluidic devices. All RL ablation methodology cited here comes
from robotics (legged locomotion) and game-playing domains. The generalizability of those
findings to HydrionEnv's continuous flow-control setting is an untested assumption.

### 8.2 Sensor-channel vs physics-channel contribution in process control RL

No published work directly studies the specific question of whether noisy sensor
measurements of flow and differential pressure are sufficient for RL-based process
control when the truth state (membrane loading, electrostatic capture efficiency, outlet
concentration) is unavailable. M8 will generate the first data point on this question
for the HydrionEnv domain.

### 8.3 Sufficiency of actuator feedback without physics truth in fouling dynamics

Whether actuator command history (valve, pump, backflush, voltage) carries sufficient
information to infer the qualitative phase of a membrane fouling cycle (loading, near-
clog, post-backflush) is theoretically plausible (actuator patterns correlate with phases)
but not established empirically. M8's result will provide indirect evidence but will not
isolate this factor.

### 8.4 Effect of calibration-pending noise on ablation validity

The calibration-pending nature of dp_drift_rate, dp_fouling_gain, and
flow_calibration_bias_std means that the sensor channel quality in obs8_deployment_v1 is
not validated. If real sensor noise is significantly different from the placeholder model,
the M8 ablation result may not generalize to the deployed system. There is no literature
directly addressing the validity of RL ablation studies conducted under placeholder noise.
This is an open methodological limitation specific to HydrOS.
